What this project is about

Every day, millions of people in Switzerland travel to work, change jobs, move to a new city, or start a family. Understanding how those individual life decisions unfold over time is the foundation of transport planning — yet the data needed to study them are rarely available.

This project builds a framework that generates entire synthetic lives: fictional but statistically realistic individuals whose birth, education, career, housing choices, and mobility habits are simulated from the ground up. Because the individuals are artificial, they can be observed at any point in time — and they carry no real personal information.

The result is a tool that transport researchers can use to model long-horizon travel demand without waiting for rare longitudinal surveys, navigating privacy restrictions, or stitching together incomplete records.

Three problems, one solution

01

Privacy

Linking real residence, employment, and income data at the individual level raises serious legal and ethical barriers. Synthetic individuals carry no personal information — they can be freely shared and studied.

02

Temporal gaps

Survey panels are collected years apart. The job change, the move, the period of inactivity that happens in between — all unobserved. A generated individual exposes its full trajectory by construction, at any calendar year.

03

Longitudinal linkage

Following the same person across registers and over a lifetime is operationally hard. Here, the entire life history is generated in one coherent pass — consistent, complete, and infinitely reproducible.

A life in five chapters

Each synthetic individual is described by five dimensions — modular building blocks that together reconstruct a coherent life story. Two complementary semester projects cover them all.

E

Existence

Birth, sex, lifespan. The temporal skeleton every other dimension is anchored to.

→
DL

Driving Licence

Whether and when a licence is obtained — a key behavioural variable for mode choice.

The key insight: because all five dimensions are generated together for the same individual, any cross-sectional observation — "where does this person live and work in 2018?" — can be reconstructed as a simple projection of a richer underlying life-course record.

Technical foundations

The following sections are aimed at researchers and students familiar with microsimulation and transport modelling.

Methodological architecture

Time-independent generation

Following Kukic, Ilinov & Bierlaire [1], individual characteristics are generated once as universal time-independent variables and subsequently mapped to time-dependent panel observations for any year of interest. This separates the simulation of the underlying life history from the observation model — enabling the same synthetic person to appear in multiple survey waves without resampling.

Event-based framework

Baud [2] formalises individual generation as a set of trajectories organised into dimensions, events, durations, and attributes. Each event is subject to temporal, biological, legal, and inter-dimension constraints — e.g. work cannot begin before education ends; residence moves are conditioned on the new place of work. This structure ensures internal coherence across the full life course.

Empirical refinements to the baseline model

The baseline specification carried several deliberate simplifications. Both reports propose targeted refinements grounded in Swiss FSO/OFS data.

Existence & Driving Licence Report 2

Sex-specific mortality tables (FSO life expectancy data) replace a single-population lifespan distribution. Driving licence acquisition is calibrated against the Swiss Microcensus on Mobility and Transport, capturing cohort and age effects that a simple Bernoulli draw cannot reproduce.

Education Report 2

The Swiss education system is modelled at a fine institutional level: compulsory schooling, vocational education and training (VET/CFC), baccalaureate schools (gymnase), specialised schools, and tertiary pathways. Completion probabilities and transition rates are drawn from official FSO statistics [7–13].

Initial residence at end of education phase P(canton0 ≠ birth canton) = f(tertiary enrolment, FSO origin-destination data [10])
Work Report 1

Labour-market entry is modelled with education-attainment-specific delays, replacing the single Gaussian used in the baseline. Employment spell durations follow an age-dependent schedule recalibrated from the U.S. NLSY [3] with Swiss corrections. Income is estimated via a Mincer-type wage equation [4] with separate education returns for secondary and tertiary graduates, consistent with the FSO Earnings Structure Survey [8].

Mincer wage equation (refined) ln(w) = α + βeduc · educ + βexp · exp + βexp² · exp² + ε

βeduc now takes distinct values for secondary and tertiary education, rather than a single return.

Residence Report 1

The constant move probability ph = 0.95 of the baseline is replaced by a career-stage-dependent schedule, following Lerch's [6] finding that residential mobility decreases monotonically over the life course. The binary urban/rural boost applied to four cantons is replaced by a continuous population-density gradient across all 26 cantons, derived from FSO cantonal statistics [9].

Residence-move probability P(move | age, career stage, Δ-canton density) = σ( γ0 + γage · age + γdensity · Δρ )

Validation strategy

Both reports evaluate the refined models by comparing projected cantonal and employment distributions against FSO/OFS cross-sections for the Swiss population. The criterion: do simulated individuals, projected onto a given observation year, reproduce the macro-statistics reported by the FSO?

  • Cantonal employment shares vs. FSO cantonal employment statistics
  • Inter-cantonal migration flows vs. Lerch [6] and FSO mobility statistics [7]
  • Income distribution by education level vs. FSO Earnings Structure Survey [8]
  • Driving licence ownership by age and sex vs. Swiss Microcensus [5, 6]
  • Educational attainment distribution vs. FSO upper-secondary completion rates [7–13]
