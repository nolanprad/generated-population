Framework

The objective of the framework is to generate synthetic individuals together with coherent life histories. Rather than generating isolated observations at a single calendar year, the model describes the successive events experienced by an individual over their lifetime. Each individual is represented through a set of trajectories—Existence, Education, Driving Licence, Work, Residence—where each trajectory describes one aspect of life.

Within each trajectory, we draw events that may or may not occur. For every event that does occur, we then generate its gap and duration, followed by any other attributes (such as sex, place of residence, or education title), each sampled according to laws taken from scientific and official sources. Events that do not occur carry no duration or attributes. A trajectory is therefore the ordered sequence of realised events, each with its timing and attributes.

The Existence trajectory has a special role: it defines the temporal support of the individual. The Birth event determines both the date of birth and the lifespan, and all other events must occur within this lifetime—an individual cannot enter tertiary education or obtain a driving licence after death. In this sense, Existence constrains every other trajectory.

Order of sampling

The Existence trajectory is sampled entirely first, since the lifespan it produces bounds the temporal support of every other event. The four remaining trajectories—Education, Driving Licence, Work, and Residence—are then sampled jointly, under laws that make them depend on one another. For instance, tertiary education influences both driving-licence acquisition and labour-market entry, and the place of residence is coupled to the place of work.

For each trajectory, the procedure is the same: first decide which events occur, then—for each realised event—draw its gap, its duration, and its remaining attributes from the specified laws. This keeps the model flexible: new trajectories or attributes can be added without changing the overall structure.

Constraints

The framework imposes several types of constraints to keep generated trajectories coherent. Structural constraints ensure that events occur in a valid order and cover the relevant part of the lifespan. Biological and legal constraints prevent unrealistic histories, such as obtaining a driving licence before the legal age. Interdependence constraints allow events from different trajectories to depend on one another.

A core architectural constraint is that the laws may only condition on time-independent universal variables already drawn earlier in the chain; conditioning directly on calendar time t is forbidden. This is what guarantees a consistent reconstruction of the individual at any year.

From time-independent to time-dependent

The generated individual is first described by time-independent variables—date of birth, lifespan, education history, licence acquisition age. Together these define a complete life history. The individual can then be observed at any calendar year by projecting this history into a time-dependent dataset: the same individual can be seen in 2000, 2010, or 2020, with age, education status, and licence status reconstructed from the underlying trajectories.

Sampling mechanics

Convex Hit-and-Run

Birth date and lifespan are drawn using a Convex Metropolis–Hastings step that uses Hit-and-Run proposals on the convex polytope defined by the temporal constraints. Hit-and-Run produces uniform local moves whose proposal density cancels out in the acceptance ratio.

Warm-up & burn-in

Two stabilisation mechanisms are used. The warm-up generates a population of individuals born before the start of the study window, so that we don't begin with an empty population on the first study year: by the time the window opens, older cohorts are already present and the age structure is realistic. The sampler also uses a burn-in phase: the initial iterations are discarded before samples are retained. Neither is used to selectively adjust the reported results.

Trajectory specifications

Existence 01

In the baseline, the Birth event is described by a date of birth drawn uniformly and a lifespan drawn from a Weibull distribution truncated at 110 years. The Weibull is used because it provides a flexible, right-skewed survival distribution concentrated around realistic lifespans.

The extended model attaches a sex attribute to the Birth event, because mortality differs systematically between men and women in Switzerland. Lifespan is then drawn conditionally on sex, with parameters calibrated so that women's expected lifespan exceeds men's, while keeping the truncation at 110 years.

Extended Existence sEx(B) ~ Uniform[1920−w, 2050]  ·  asex(B) ~ Bernoulli(pf = 0.5)
dEx(B) | sex = s ~ Weibull(ks, λs) on (0, 110]

Education 02

The Education trajectory is modelled as a hierarchical sequence of events rather than a direct assignment of a final level. Mandatory schooling is deterministic and compulsory: it starts at age 4 and lasts 11 years, so individuals complete it around age 15.

After mandatory education, individuals enter one of three mutually exclusive outcomes—professional secondary education (PSE, leading to a CFC), academic secondary education (ASE, a specialised-school or baccalaureate diploma), or none. The proportions reflect the dominant role of vocational training in Switzerland.

Secondary pathway · tertiary access SE | mandatory ~ Categorical(PPSE=0.67, PASE=0.23, PNone=0.10)
iTE | SE = ASE ~ Bernoulli(0.90)

Tertiary access is restricted to the academic pathway, reflecting the institutional constraint that universities generally require an academic qualification. Tertiary students are then assigned an institution type (UNI/EPF vs HES/HEP) and a degree level (Bachelor vs Master), with nominal durations of 3 years (Bachelor) and 5 years cumulative (Master), allowed to vary within bounded intervals. A highest-diploma attribute is exported for use by other trajectories.

Driving Licence 03

The trajectory describes the transition between two states, "no driving licence" and "driving licence". Every individual starts without a licence; once obtained, ownership is assumed irreversible. In the baseline, the acquisition probability is constant for everyone surviving past the legal age of 18, and the acquisition age follows a truncated log-normal concentrated shortly after that age.

The extended model introduces two refinements. First, the probability of obtaining a licence depends on sex and on tertiary-education participation—the latter capturing delayed or less frequent acquisition among students. Second, the acquisition age is allowed to differ by sex.

Heterogeneous acquisition P(iDL = 1 | sex = s, TE = u) = ps · ψu
pfemale = 0.78,   pmale = 0.86  ·  ψ0 = 1,   ψ1 = 0.90

Conditional on acquisition, the age follows a sex-specific truncated log-normal on [18, min(70, lifespan)], slightly later and more dispersed for women, consistent with observed Swiss mobility statistics.

Work 04

The Work trajectory is a sequence of employment spells separated by gaps, fitting within an active window that ends at age 70 or at death. Labour-market entry age is conditioned on education; each spell duration is drawn from an age-dependent truncated exponential, capped by an explicit residual-budget bound so the career never overruns the active window; income follows a Mincer equation with separate returns to secondary and tertiary education.

Entry · spell duration · income aentry ~ N(μ(SE,TE), 1.5²),   μ ∈ {16.5, 18.0, 23.0}
dWk ~ Exp(mean = d̄(ak)) on [0, Uk],   d̄ ∈ {1.5, 2.5, 3.5, 4.5, 9.0}
ln(inck) ~ N(β₀ + β₁·work + β₂·work² + ρSE·SE + ρTE·TE, σ²)

The place of work uses a four-class density-based multiplier δ(c) ∈ {2.0, 1.5, 1.2, 1.0}; inter-canton mobility follows a decreasing schedule X(k) from 30% to 5% by spell rank, with Mk ~ Bernoulli(X(k)).

Residence 05

Residence spells are aligned to work spells. The birth canton is drawn proportional to cantonal population. A single relocation opportunity at tertiary entry can move students to the canton of their institution. During employment, the home canton follows the work canton through the same mobility indicator Mk drawn in the Work trajectory, with an alignment probability conditioned on the geographical relationship between the new workplace and the previous home.

Birth canton · geography-aware alignment P(cB = c) = Nc / ΣNc'
P(homek = workk | Mk=1, k≥2) = 0.50 if neighbouring,   0.90 otherwise

Production runs

Time-independent comparisons are computed on a cohort of 50,000 individuals; projections onto reference calendar years use 100,000 individuals. All reported results are computed on post-burn-in draws of the Hit-and-Run chain.
